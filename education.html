Thesis: Intelligent Energy Monitoring and Management in Residential Grids
Supervisors: Dr. Ranjana Sodhi and Dr. Balwinder Sodhi

The primary objective of this thesis is to develop the energy management strategies to reduce the supply and demand imbalance in real-time owing to its vitality in stable and reliable operations of the power system. Traditionally, this task is accomplished by managing the supply. At times, when controlling generation is not feasible or not economically viable, blanket load-shedding of one or more large areas is often opted for. This leads to significant incon-veniences for the end-users. To handle the problem effectively, this thesis proposes an appliance-wise energy consumption management strategy that considers the end-users' preferences and compensates for the inconveniences. To this end, two types of tariff environments are explored, viz. traditional inclining block rate tariffs and dynamic pricing tariffs. The former is suitable for the present developing region context, and the latter envisages the futuristic scenario. To enable this appliance-wise energy management, initially, two appliance-wise energy consumption monitoring strategies are developed in this thesis, viz., non-intrusive and semi-intrusive load monitoring for old and new residences. To ensure practicality, importance is given to cost, accuracy and end-users' conveniences, and the efficacy of these claims is validated from simulation and lab-scale testbed results.

The primary objective of this thesis is to develop the energy management strategies to reduce the supply and demand imbalance in real-time owing to its vitality in stable and reliable operations of the power system. Traditionally, the process is carried out by controlling the centralized generation, which is expensive or not realisable at times, leading to a wide-scale load shedding. Recently, participation of end-users as an active player has brought a significant paradigm shift in the power sector; this shift is realised in the developed regions due to the availability of advanced information and communication (ICT) infrastructure and energy-aware consumers. In the developing regions, like India, the infrastructure is not fully connected, penetration of rooftop-PV is insufficient, and the residences in these regions are subjected to a non-responsive inclining block rate (IBR) tariff system. Moreover, the market doesn't exist for distribution consumers. For the residences semi-regulated environments, this work has proposed an energy management (EM) model for both grid-connected and isolated-mode of operations to quickly and fairly manage the local supply-demand mismatch. The participation strategy suggested in the proposed EM model also enables the utility (via an aggregator) to exploit the flexibility offered by residential loads in peak demand hours on a contract basis. The work also highlights a fair incentive evaluation scheme and application of residential energy storage systems (ESS) in handling the uncertainties. Moreover, real-time load monitoring mechanisms, an indispensable element of energy management, are developed for both old-retrofitted and brand-new residences, considering socio-economic compulsions. Again, for a futuristic scenario, where the regulatory framework for time-varying pricing and distribution-level market is relaxed, a multi-round ADMM-based decentralised pool market model is developed for the consumers' community to trade their flexibility via multiple aggregators to reduce the mismatch quickly and purchase power at a fairer price.
